# China Macro-Financial Pressure-Valve Framework

Use this reference when analyzing China's macro-financial stress, systemic-risk containment, RMB exchange-rate pressure, property stress, local debt, banks, employment and income, capital controls, stock-market stabilization, or ordinary-resident risk transfer.

This is a pressure-management framework, not a deterministic collapse model. Treat each valve as a place where stress can be absorbed, hidden, transferred, socialized, or released. A crisis warning becomes stronger when level stress, first-derivative deterioration, and second-derivative acceleration appear across multiple connected valves.

## Core Map

Two core valves usually dominate:

- RMB exchange rate: external confidence, capital-flight expectation, import cost, and monetary-sovereignty constraint.
- Property market: household balance sheet, bank collateral, local fiscal capacity, and credit-creation engine.

Five transmission and absorption valves usually carry redistributed pressure:

- Local debt and urban investment vehicles: fiscal continuation, infrastructure cash flow, contractor arrears, and hidden debt rollover.
- Banking system: final reservoir for credit losses, margin compression, maturity extension, and forced consolidation.
- Employment and income: consumption willingness, social-stability pressure, mortgage-servicing capacity, and household risk tolerance.
- Capital controls: leak prevention, FX quota enforcement, outbound-investment control, and foreign-capital channel management.
- Stock and capital markets: wealth-effect management, corporate financing, policy narrative, institutional exit, and retail risk absorption.

## Valve Dashboard

Score each valve from 0 to 5:

| Score | Meaning |
| ---: | --- |
| 0 | Benign or improving. |
| 1 | Watch item; mild level pressure or isolated deterioration. |
| 2 | Persistent first-derivative deterioration. |
| 3 | Second-derivative acceleration in the valve itself. |
| 4 | Cross-valve transmission is visible. |
| 5 | Stress is near systemic; ordinary residents face rule-change, liquidity, or loss-socialization risk. |

For each valve, separate:

- Level: current severity versus history, official thresholds, and household tolerance.
- First derivative: whether the latest 1-week, 1-month, or 1-quarter change is worsening.
- Second derivative: whether deterioration is accelerating, broadening, or spreading into adjacent valves.
- Policy response: which tools are being used and whether they are liquidity repair, solvency repair, confidence management, or control tightening.
- Pressure-transfer direction: which valve is absorbing stress next.
- Resident exposure: worker, saver, borrower, homeowner, investor, taxpayer, pension contributor.

## Seven Valves

### 1. RMB Exchange Rate

What it controls:

- Capital-flight expectation, external confidence, import inflation, monetary-policy room, and domestic asset repricing.

Watch:

- USD/CNY and USD/CNH trend; CNH-CNY spread; daily fixing gap; FX-reserve changes; bank FX settlement/sales; SAFE enforcement; export proceeds conversion; foreign-investor bond/equity flows; offshore dollar funding; gold and USD household demand.

Policy tools:

- Stronger fixing, counter-cyclical factor, state-bank dollar selling, macro-prudential parameters, FX scrutiny, outbound remittance proof, capital-account restrictions, verbal guidance, and managed opening to selected foreign capital.

Danger signals:

- CNY weakens while trade surplus remains strong.
- CNH persistently weaker than CNY and fixing credibility weakens.
- FX controls tighten in small operational details before headline quota changes.
- Residents and firms accelerate legal or gray-channel dollarization.

Resident read:

- Exchange-rate stress often appears first as stricter documentation, slower approvals, and wider spreads rather than official quota cuts. Do not assume formal quotas equal usable exit rights.

### 2. Property Market

What it controls:

- Household asset balance sheets, bank collateral value, developer solvency, land finance, local fiscal capacity, and construction employment.

Watch:

- New-home and second-hand prices; transaction volume; inventory months; land-auction premium and failed auctions; developer bond/default status; mortgage rates and prepayment; household leverage; foreclosure and auction listings; bank mortgage/NPL exposure.

Policy tools:

- Purchase restrictions relaxation, mortgage-rate cuts, down-payment cuts, whitelist financing, guaranteed delivery funding, local SOE acquisition of inventory, urban village renovation, and local housing subsidies.

Danger signals:

- Volume fails to recover after price and credit easing.
- Existing-home prices fall faster than new-home prices.
- Local land revenue remains weak despite policy support.
- Support protects developers and banks more than household resale liquidity.

Resident read:

- Policy support can prevent visible collapse without restoring household exit liquidity. For ordinary residents, resale depth matters more than official price stability.

### 3. Local Debt and Urban Investment Vehicles

What it controls:

- Local fiscal continuation, infrastructure employment, hidden debt rollover, public-service funding, contractor payment, and local bank asset quality.

Watch:

- Land-transfer revenue; local fiscal deficit; special refinancing bond quota; LGFV bond spreads and maturity wall; non-standard debt rollover; contractor arrears; public-service fees; local SOE asset transfers; city-level population and tax base.

Policy tools:

- Debt swaps, special refinancing bonds, policy-bank lending, LGFV consolidation, project asset injection, fiscal transfer payments, fee/tax increases, payment extensions, and implicit guarantee reshaping.

Danger signals:

- Debt swaps lower interest cost but no operating cash flow appears.
- Contractor arrears, wage arrears, or local fee increases spread.
- Weak regions rely on repeated rollover rather than fiscal recovery.
- Local banks and LGFVs become one balance sheet.

Resident read:

- Local stress is often socialized through fees, utilities, enforcement intensity, delayed payments, reduced services, and employment weakness rather than explicit default.

### 4. Banking System

What it controls:

- Credit creation, deposit safety perception, maturity transformation, credit-loss absorption, and final macro-risk reservoir.

Watch:

- Net interest margin; NPL and special-mention loans; loan-extension policies; local-bank mergers; capital adequacy; interbank/CD rates; deposit competition; wealth-management product drawdowns; trust exposure; bank equity/credit spread performance.

Policy tools:

- RRR cuts, relending, collateral expansion, capital injection, special bonds for bank recapitalization, merger of weak banks into stronger banks, loan forbearance, deposit-rate guidance, and window guidance.

Danger signals:

- Recapitalization and mergers accelerate while asset quality disclosure remains calm.
- Deposit rates are suppressed while residents are pushed toward risk products.
- Wealth-management or trust losses are delayed, restructured, or opaque.
- Small banks lose independent survival capacity.

Resident read:

- State support usually protects payment continuity and systemic stability first, not every product's principal. Keep simple deposits separate from wealth-management and insurance-like products.

### 5. Employment and Income

What it controls:

- Household repayment ability, consumption, social stability, youth expectations, entrepreneurship, and willingness to take investment risk.

Watch:

- Urban surveyed unemployment; youth unemployment methodology changes; wage growth; recruitment data; working hours; wage arrears; platform/gig income; civil-service and SOE pay delays; household income versus debt service; social-security contribution enforcement.

Policy tools:

- Public-sector hiring, vocational training, subsidies, trade-in programs, consumption vouchers, tax/fee relief, social-security enforcement, delayed retirement, and campaign-style employment support.

Danger signals:

- Income expectations weaken while credit or pension contribution pressure rises.
- Consumption stimulus relies on replacement demand rather than income growth.
- Social-security enforcement increases during weak private-sector cash flow.
- Households increase precautionary savings despite lower deposit rates.

Resident read:

- Employment and income are where macro stress becomes household life pressure. A resident should protect emergency liquidity before seeking policy-trade upside.

### 6. Capital Controls

What it controls:

- Leakage of domestic liquidity, FX reserves, confidence, external narrative, and the ability to maintain domestic monetary conditions.

Watch:

- FX quota usability; proof-of-use requirements; overseas investment approvals; QDII quota; northbound/southbound flows; corporate overseas listing scrutiny; cross-border payment enforcement; export under-invoicing/over-invoicing signals; bank compliance campaigns.

Policy tools:

- Documentation tightening, anti-money-laundering checks, outbound-investment approval, QDII quota adjustment, foreign-investor access campaigns, targeted opening, and enforcement threats.

Danger signals:

- Formal rules remain unchanged but operational friction rises.
- Small transfers face more proof burden.
- Foreign inflow channels are promoted while resident outflow channels become harder.
- Legal uncertainty increases around ordinary FX use.

Resident read:

- In capital-control stress, exit rights are operational, not just legal. Ordinary residents should avoid relying on last-minute conversion or transfer plans.

### 7. Stock and Capital Markets

What it controls:

- Wealth effect, household risk appetite, corporate refinancing, pledge risk, institutional exit, technological self-reliance narrative, and confidence management.

Watch:

- Broad-index trend versus median stock returns; ETF share changes; state-fund or policy-tool activity; IPO/refinancing pace; margin financing; turnover concentration; pension/insurance/fund guidance; dividend and buyback policies; delisting and investor compensation; retail account inflows.

Policy tools:

- ETF buying, refinancing controls, IPO pacing, buyback/reloan tools, insurance and pension allocation guidance, short-selling restrictions, transaction-cost changes, state-media confidence campaigns, and thematic industrial narratives.

Danger signals:

- Indices stay stable while median stocks fall.
- Broad ETF shares contract while index levels are defended.
- Turnover concentrates in a few policy or technology themes.
- Retail inflows appear after institutional exit channels improve.
- Investor protection language lacks enforceable compensation, disclosure, litigation, or delisting mechanisms.

Resident read:

- Capital-market support can create tradable rallies, but ordinary residents should ask who gets liquidity and who gets lock-up or final risk. Treat policy bull markets as tactical risk, not a guarantee of household wealth repair.

## Cross-Valve Transmission Chains

Map stress as a chain:

```text
Property price/volume stress
-> land finance weakens
-> local debt rollover pressure
-> local banks and LGFVs absorb losses
-> bank margins/capital weaken
-> residents face lower deposit yield, product substitution, fees, and employment pressure
-> capital-market wealth-effect tools are used
-> RMB/capital-control pressure rises if confidence weakens
```

Common transfer directions:

- Property -> local debt -> banks -> fiscal fees and household burden.
- RMB pressure -> capital controls -> domestic asset lock-in -> capital-market policy support.
- Employment weakness -> consumption weakness -> corporate profits -> stock-market confidence management.
- Bank stress -> deposit-rate suppression -> residents pushed into wealth products, insurance, pensions, or equity funds.
- Local debt stress -> public fee/utility increases -> resident disposable-income squeeze.

## Policy Tool Classification

When a policy appears, classify it:

| Tool type | What it usually does | Main risk |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Liquidity repair | Prevents immediate funding break. | Can delay solvency recognition. |
| Solvency repair | Injects capital, writes down debt, or transfers losses. | Identifies who pays. |
| Confidence management | Supports prices, narratives, or expectations. | Can pull residents into risk before fundamentals repair. |
| Control tightening | Reduces exit or arbitrage. | Preserves system liquidity but lowers household optionality. |
| Loss socialization | Moves cost to savers, taxpayers, workers, investors, or future retirees. | Often hidden in rates, fees, inflation, lock-up, or lower services. |

## Regime Classification

Use these macro-financial states:

- Controlled compression: several valves are stressed, but policy tools prevent visible breakage; residents experience lower returns, higher fees, weaker income, and reduced optionality.
- Pressure rotation: one valve is relieved by shifting stress to another, such as property risk moving to banks or capital-market risk moving to retail investors.
- Liquidity rescue: policy tools supply liquidity, but solvency losses remain unresolved.
- Loss socialization: households, taxpayers, pension contributors, or retail investors increasingly absorb costs.
- Hard-break risk: multiple valves show second-derivative deterioration and policy tools no longer stabilize confidence.

## Ordinary-Resident Checklist

Use this checklist after the macro diagnosis:

- Keep emergency liquidity outside long lock-up products.
- Separate deposits from bank wealth management, insurance savings, bond funds, and pension accounts.
- Avoid chasing policy-driven rallies without exit rules.
- Treat high yield from weak local institutions as compensation for hidden risk, not a free lunch.
- Evaluate housing by local income, population, fiscal capacity, and resale depth, not national policy slogans.
- Do not rely on last-minute FX conversion when operational capital-control friction is rising.
- Watch local fee, utility, social-security, and public-service changes as direct household-risk transfer signals.


Always distinguish evidence from inference. Do not describe a systemic break as inevitable unless multiple valves show confirmed second-derivative acceleration and policy response is failing.
